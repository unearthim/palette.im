Google Reader

2005 – 2013

"The single most mourned death in the history of the open web. Killed to force Google+."

On July 1, 2013, the internet lost something irreplaceable. Google Reader wasn't just an RSS aggregator—it was the nervous system of the open web. That orange icon represented curiosity, curation, and the promise that you could follow any site, anywhere, without algorithms deciding what you saw. Google killed it to boost Google+. It didn't work. The wound never healed.

2005 — Original Launch

The Orange Era

That warm, confident orange—the color of RSS itself. Paired with clean whites and Google's signature red accent, it was functional minimalism at its peak. No clutter. Just the web, organized.

2011 — The Redesign

Red Shift

The controversial redesign. Google pushed its unified design language—more red, less orange. Users hated it. The compact list view was gone. This was the beginning of the end, though nobody knew it yet.

2013 — Sunset

The Greying

March 13, 2013: Google announced the shutdown. July 1, 2013: The feeds stopped. The orange was gone. The web got a little dimmer. Power users scrambled to export their OPML files. An era ended.
