Vine

2013 – 2017

"Six-second art form. Twitter couldn't monetize it. The loops stopped."

Vine invented a new art form: six-second looping videos. Creators learned to tell complete jokes, build narratives, and craft perfect moments in 360 frames. That teal-green interface became the color of internet culture from 2013-2016. But Twitter couldn't figure out how to make money from it. Meanwhile, Musical.ly (later TikTok) studied Vine's success. When Twitter shut it down in 2017, TikTok inherited the empire. Vine died so TikTok could live.

2013 — Launch

Teal Dreams

That fresh, vibrant teal-green. Not Twitter's blue, not Instagram's gradient—Vine carved out its own visual identity. Clean white backgrounds, simple interface. The color said: "This is new. This is different. This is creativity unleashed."

2015-2016 — Peak Era

Darker Teal, Bigger Dreams

At its cultural peak, Vine subtly darkened the teal. Stars emerged—King Bach, Lele Pons, Thomas Sanders. The interface got minor tweaks but kept its signature color. This was Vine as cultural force, the palette of memes and viral moments. But Twitter was losing patience.

2017 — Shutdown

The Loop Stopped

October 27, 2016: Twitter announced the shutdown. January 17, 2017: The app stopped working. The teal icon disappeared from phones. Millions of Vines were archived, but the platform—the creative energy, the community—was gone. TikTok rose from its ashes.
