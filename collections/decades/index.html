Decades

Every era had its palette. From neon rebellion to flat minimalism, these are the colors that defined digital culture.

Color trends don't emerge in a vacuum—they're responses to technology, cultural moments, and collective mood. A decade's palette tells you what was possible, what was fashionable, and what people were feeling. This is the visual language of the internet, decade by decade.

1990s

"Under Construction"

Neon rebellion against beige corporate design. Background textures everywhere. Animated GIFs. The web was a playground, and color was free. If Netscape could render it, someone would use it—proudly.

Cultural Context

The 216-color "web-safe" palette dominated. Limited by monitors that could only display 256 colors, designers embraced bold primaries and eye-searing combinations. Tiled background images (often marble or stars) were a status symbol. The aesthetic was "more is more"—dancing babies, spinning logos, hit counters. This was the internet's adolescence: chaotic, experimental, unapologetically loud.

2000-2005

"Web 2.0 Gloss"

Glossy buttons. Reflective surfaces. Gradients that screamed "professional." Blue was king. This was the era of "design maturity"—the web growing up, getting venture capital, and putting on a suit.

Cultural Context

Ajax changed everything. Sites became "applications." Trust was blue (Facebook, Twitter, PayPal, Skype). Orange meant "action" (RSS feeds, Blogger, Firefox). Rounded corners, drop shadows, and glossy effects were everywhere—the skeuomorphic aesthetic that mimicked real-world materials. This was peak optimism: the web was going to democratize everything.

2010s

"Flat Design Minimalism"

Death to gradients. iOS 7 killed skeuomorphism and ushered in an era of flat, vibrant simplicity. Pastel backgrounds. Sans-serif everything. The palette became a brand identity statement.

Cultural Context

Mobile-first design demanded simplicity. Retina displays made subtlety possible. Every startup had a single-color brand (Airbnb coral, Slack aubergine, Spotify green). Gradients returned, but softer—pastel washes instead of glossy shine. Dark mode emerged. Accessibility mattered. The palette became restrained, intentional, corporate. Beauty with guardrails.

2020s

"Brutalist Nostalgia"

Y2K revival meets dark mode maximalism. Neon accents on black. Glitch aesthetics. Hyper-saturated gradients. The web is remembering what it forgot—that color can be fun, weird, rebellious again.

Cultural Context

Gen Z discovered GeoCities through Internet Archive and said "this rules, actually." Designers rejected minimalism for maximalist energy. Neon on black became the aesthetic of crypto, gaming, and rebellion. Glitch effects, distorted text, intentionally "broken" design. The palette is high-contrast, highly saturated, and unapologetically chaotic. We've come full circle—90s energy with modern tools.
