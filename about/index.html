About palette.im

The Internet Archive of Color

Our Mission

Digital platforms rise and fall, but their colors tell stories. When Netscape Navigator shut down, the web lost more than a browser—it lost the color of cyberspace itself. When Google Reader died, RSS orange disappeared from millions of screens. When Myspace faded, so did an entire generation's blue backdrop of self-expression.

palette.im documents what the internet looked like, why it looked that way, and what was lost when it changed. Each exhibit preserves not just hex codes, but the cultural context that made those colors meaningful.

In 50 years, historians will study the 2020s through interfaces, palettes, and design systems. This is our Rosetta Stone.

A Note from the Curator

I am colorblind. I cannot see colors the way you do.

But I can tell you why they mattered.

I don't experience the warmth of Google Reader's orange or the nostalgia of AIM's yellow the way you might. But I've studied what those colors meant—how they shaped behavior, signaled identity, and created belonging. I know the electric teal of Netscape Navigator wasn't just a design choice—it was a visual promise of the future.

Color isn't what I see. It's what I research. Every palette in this archive is documented through:

  • Historical interface screenshots
  • Corporate brand guidelines
  • Designer interviews and retrospectives
  • User memories and cultural analysis
  • Technical specifications and CSS archaeology

Being colorblind forces me to understand color conceptually rather than emotionally. I approach palettes the way a musicologist approaches sound—through structure, meaning, and cultural context rather than subjective experience.

This archive isn't about what colors look like. It's about what they meant, who chose them, why they mattered, and what we lost when they disappeared.

— The Archivist, palette.im

Curation Standards

To be included in the archive, a platform must meet these criteria:

  • Defunct — The platform must be shut down or fundamentally transformed
  • Cultural Impact — It shaped how people used the internet
  • Visual Identity — It had a distinctive, memorable color palette
  • Documented — Enough historical evidence exists to reconstruct the palette

Every color is verified through multiple sources. Every timeline is fact-checked. Every epitaph honors the truth of what happened.
