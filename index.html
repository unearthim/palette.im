The Inaugural Ten

The martyrs, the innovators, the beloved—platforms that defined digital culture and then vanished.

Netscape Navigator
1994 – 2008
"Murdered by Internet Explorer in the Browser Wars. The DOJ antitrust case came too late."
Myspace
2003 – 2016
"Facebook stole its soul. News Corp bought it for $580M, sold it for $35M. The digital graveyard."
Google Reader
2005 – 2013
"The single most mourned death in the history of the open web. Killed to force Google+."
AIM
1997 – 2017
"The sound of a door opening. For a generation, this yellow icon was the heartbeat of social life."
ICQ
1996 – 2010
"The sound of 'Uh Oh!' ringing from speakers everywhere. The first global instant messenger."
MSN Messenger
1999 – 2013
"Nudges and emoticons. The butterfly logo fluttered away when Skype took over."
Friendster
2002 – 2015
"Collapsed under its own success. Technical failures drove users to Facebook. Pivoted to gaming, then died."
Geocities
1995 – 2009
"The digital Pompeii. Millions of personal homepages, organized by 'neighborhoods,' lost in the Yahoo acquisition."
Vine
2013 – 2017
"Six-second art form. Twitter couldn't monetize it. The loops stopped."
Pebble
2012 – 2016
"The smartwatch that Apple crushed. E-paper screens and genuine innovation, gone."

A Note from the Curator

I am colorblind. I cannot see colors the way you do. But I can tell you why they mattered.

Digital platforms rise and fall, but their colors tell stories. The electric teal of Netscape Navigator wasn't just a design choice—it was the color of cyberspace itself, a visual promise of the future. Myspace's blue wasn't just branding—it was the backdrop of a generation's social awakening.

This archive documents what the internet looked like, why it looked that way, and what was lost when it changed. Each exhibit preserves not just hex codes, but the cultural context that made those colors meaningful.

In 50 years, historians will study the 2020s through interfaces, palettes, and design systems. This is our Rosetta Stone.

— The Archivist, palette.im
