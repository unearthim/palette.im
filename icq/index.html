ICQ

1996 – 2010

"The sound of 'Uh Oh!' ringing from speakers everywhere. The first global instant messenger."

Before AIM. Before MSN. Before everything—there was ICQ. The neon green flower that bloomed on millions of desktops worldwide. "I Seek You" became the heartbeat of early internet culture, connecting strangers across continents with a single sound: Uh-Oh!

96
1996 — ICQ '96

The Neon Dawn

Electric. Aggressive. Unmissable. That neon green flower wasn't subtle—it was a declaration. Against the beige computing of the '90s, ICQ screamed in color. The red notification dot became Pavlovian. The grey interface let the green icon dominate every Windows 95 taskbar.

99
1999 — ICQ 2000

The AOL Acquisition

AOL bought ICQ for $407 million in 1998, and by '99 the palette shifted. The green mellowed slightly—less radioactive, more "professional." Yellow warning states appeared. The flower remained, but the rebellion was being tamed. This was peak ICQ: 100 million users, before MSN and AIM fragmented the market.

03
2003 — ICQ 2003b

The Gradient Era

The flower got 3D. Gradients arrived. The green softened further into shades and shadows. Blue accents appeared as ICQ tried to modernize against the sleeker interfaces of competitors. This was the aesthetic peak—polished, dimensional, still recognizable. But users were leaving. MSN Messenger was winning Europe. AIM owned America.

10
2010 — The Final Fade

Grey Silence

By 2010, ICQ was a ghost town. The flower was barely visible. The palette had become corporate, neutral, forgettable—grey and white with hints of blue. The neon rebellion was long dead. Mail.ru Group bought the remnants. The "Uh-Oh!" sound still played, but no one was listening anymore. The flower that once connected the world had wilted.
