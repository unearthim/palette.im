Geocities

1995 – 2009

"The digital Pompeii. Millions of personal homepages, organized by 'neighborhoods,' lost in the Yahoo acquisition."

Before social media, before blogs, there was Geocities. You picked a neighborhood—SiliconValley for tech, SunsetStrip for music, Area51 for sci-fi—and built a homepage with <blink> tags, animated GIFs, tiled backgrounds, and visitor counters. It was chaotic, ugly, beautiful, and deeply human. In 2009, Yahoo deleted it all. Millions of sites—art, journals, fan pages, family archives—gone. The Internet Archive saved what it could, but an entire era of digital folk art vanished.

1995-1999 — The Wild West

Every Color at Once

There was no official Geocities palette because every site was different. Bright yellows, electric blues, hot pinks, lime greens—often on the same page. Starry backgrounds. Rainbow dividers. Flaming text. This was the web before design rules, before taste, before algorithms. It was glorious.

1999-2009 — Yahoo Purple

Corporate Branding

Yahoo bought Geocities for $3.57 billion. The wild yellows and pinks were replaced with Yahoo's purple and grey. The login page became corporate. The neighborhoods were deemphasized. The platform stagnated. Users stopped updating their sites. The color drained away.

2009 — Deletion

Black Screen

October 26, 2009: Yahoo shut it down. Millions of pages—deleted. The Internet Archive's "Wayback Machine" saved fragments, but most of it is gone. Geocities was never profitable. It was cultural history, and Yahoo erased it for server space. All those colors, all that creativity—black.
